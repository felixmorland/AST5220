\section{Background cosmology}
To compute the CMB power spectrum, we must first establish the background cosmological model upon which perturbations will evolve. This section details the construction of this background Universe. We begin by outlining the theoretical framework, most notably the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric and the resulting Friedmann equations that governs the expansion history and the evolution of its energy components. Subsequently, we describe the numerical implementation of this model, including the fiducial parameters adopted from Planck 2018. Finally, we validate our implementation by comparing its predicted timeline of cosmic epochs and derived quantities, such as the age of the universe, against theoretical expectations, and we present the results of an MCMC parameter fit to supernova data, which contextualizes the well-known Hubble tension within our model.

\subsubsection{The Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker geometry}

For our background cosmology, we assume an isotropic and homogeneous Universe with a spatial scale factor $a(t)$ equipped with the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric

\begin{align}
      ds^2 = -dt^2 + a^2(t)\Big(dx^2+dy^2+dz^2\Big),\label{eq:FLRW metric}
\end{align}
or, on component form; $g_{00}=-1$, $g_{ij} = a^2(t)\delta_{ij}$.

At the background level, the constituents of our Universe, which include radiation (photons and massless neutrinos), non-relativistic matter (baryons and cold dark matter), and a cosmological constant, are treated as perfect fluids. For each component $i$, the stress-energy tensor in the comoving frame takes the diagonal form
\begin{equation*}
T_{\mu\nu}^{(i)} = \mathrm{diag}(\rho_i, p_i, p_i, p_i),
\end{equation*}
with $\rho_i$ denoting the density and $p_i$ the pressure of that component.

The resulting Einstein field equations for the FLRW metric and the sum of the stress-energy tensors for the perfect fluids,
\begin{align*}
      R_{\mu\nu}-\frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\sum_i T^{(i)}_{\mu\nu},
\end{align*}
we get the Friedmann equations. The (00) component of the Einstein equations gives us the first equation,
\begin{align}
      H^2(a) \equiv \bigg(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\bigg)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\sum_i \rho_i,\label{eq:Friedmann 1st}
\end{align}
while the (11), (22) and (33) components gives us the second Friedmann equation (assuming isotropy, or else they would yield three different equations),  
\begin{align}
      \frac{\ddot{a}}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\sum_i (\rho_i + 3p_i). \label{eq:Friedmann 2nd}
\end{align}
As our time variable, we will use the logarithm of scale factor $x\equiv \ln [a(t)]$. We thus need to study the Friedmann equations, Eqs.(\ref{eq:Friedmann 1st})-(\ref{eq:Friedmann 2nd}), to understand how the scale factor evolves and how it is related to the evolution of the densities of the different components in our model Universe.

\subsubsection{The Hubble function}
Introducing the definition of the density parameters $\Omega_i \equiv 8\pi G \rho_i/3H^2$, and the equation of state $w_i \equiv p_i/\rho_i$, the first Friedmann equation can be recast into the form we will be using for our implementation of the background cosmology
\begin{align*}
      H^2 = H_0^2\sum_i \frac{\Omega_{i0}}{a^{3(1+w_i)}},
\end{align*}
where $H_0 = 100h\; \text{km/s/Mpc}$ is the Hubble constant, and $\Omega_{i0}$ is the current day value of the density parameter of component $i$.

In our model Universe, we have dark energy, described by a cosmological constant ($\Lambda$) with equation of state $w_\Lambda=-1$, non-relativistic matter in the form of baryons ($b$) and cold dark matter (CDM) with equation of state $w_M = 0$, and two relativistic species, photons ($\gamma$) and neutrinos ($\nu$) with $w_R = 1/3$. The curvature (k) can be decribed as having an equation of state $w_k = -1/3$. We can thus explicitly write out our Hubble function as
\begin{equation}
      H(a) = H_0 \sqrt{\Omega_{\Lambda 0} + \frac{\Omega_{k 0}}{a^2} + \frac{\Omega_{b0} + \Omega_{\rm CDM 0}}{a^3} + \frac{\Omega_{\gamma 0} +\Omega_{\nu 0}}{a^4}}.\label{eq:Hubble a}
\end{equation}

The evolution of the density parameters as functions of the scale factor are thus given by
\begin{align}
\Omega_{k}(a) &= \frac{\Omega_{K0}}{a^2H(a)^2/H_0^2},\quad\quad\Omega_{M}(a) = \frac{\Omega_{M0}}{a^3H(a)^2/H_0^2},\nonumber \\
\Omega_R(a) &= \frac{\Omega_{R 0}}{a^4H(a)^2/H_0^2}, \quad\quad\Omega_{\Lambda}(a) = \frac{\Omega_{\Lambda 0}}{H(a)^2/H_0^2}.\label{eq:density parameter evolutions}
\end{align}
Here we define the non-relativistic matter density parameter $\Omega_M = \Omega_b + \Omega_\text{CDM}$ and the relativistic species density parameter $\Omega_R = \Omega_\gamma + \Omega_\nu$.


\subsubsection{Cosmic and conformal time}

In the FLRW metric, Eq.(\ref{eq:FLRW metric}), we use the \textit{cosmic time} coordinate $t$ which is related to the scale factor through the Hubble function through the relation
\begin{align*}
      H \equiv \frac{1}{a}\dd{a}{t}\;\Rightarrow \; \dd{t}{x} =\frac{1}{H}, 
\end{align*}
or on integral form 
\begin{align}
      t(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \frac{\d x'}{H(x')} .\label{eq:t(x)}
\end{align}

However, we also consider the \textit{conformal time} $\eta$ which is the distance that light may have travelled since the Big Bang (horizon). It is related to the cosmic time thorugh the relation
\begin{equation*}
      a\d \eta = c\d t \;\Rightarrow\; \dd{\eta}{x} = \frac{c}{aH}.
\end{equation*}
For calculating $\eta$, it is also prudent to introduce the conformal Hubble function $\mathcal{H}\equiv \frac{1}{a}\dd{a}{\eta}=aH $ such that the conformal time can be written on integral form as
\begin{equation}
      \eta(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x}\frac{c\d x'}{\mathcal{H}(x')}.\label{eq:eta(x)}
\end{equation}
The derivatives of $\mathcal{H}$ with respect to $x$ are given by;
\begin{align}
      \dd{\mathcal{H}}{x} &= \mathcal{H}\bigg[1- \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{M} -2\Omega_{R} - \Omega_K\bigg] \;\Bigg|_{a = e^x}\;,\label{eq:dHpdx}\\[5mm]
      \dd{^2\mathcal{H}}{x^2} &=\; \mathcal{H}\Bigg\{\bigg[1-\frac{3}{2}\Omega_M -2\Omega_R  -\Omega_K\bigg]^2\label{eq:ddHpddx}\\
      & \quad-\frac{1}{2}\Big(3\Omega_M + 4\Omega_R + 2\Omega_K \Big)^2+\frac{1}{2}\Big(9\Omega_M +16\Omega_R +4\Omega_K\Big)\Bigg\} \;\Bigg|_{a = e^x}\;,\nonumber
\end{align}
where $\Omega_i$ are the density parameters evaluated at $a=e^x$.
The derivatives of $\mathcal{H}$ are derived in Appendix \ref{appendix:derivatives of Hp}.


\subsubsection{Luminosity distance and angular diameter distance}
In order to compare our model with observations, and be able to perform parameter fitting, we will need to calculate two observables; namely the \textit{angular diameter distance} $d_A$, and the \textit{luminosity distance} $d_L$.

Writing the line element in the FLRW metric, Eq.(\ref{eq:FLRW metric}), using spherical coordinates, we have 
\begin{equation}
      \d s^2 = -c^2\d t^2 + a^2\bigg(\frac{\d r^2}{1-kr^2} + r^2\d\theta^2 + r^2\sin\theta^2\d\phi^2\bigg),\label{eq:FLRW in polar coords}
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the curvature. Since we are observing photons (that move radially along the line of sight) when determining astrophysical distances, we consider the null geodesic, $\d s^2=0$, followed by the rays of light. For these photons, we get 
\begin{equation}
      c\d t = \frac{a\d r}{\sqrt{1-kr}}\;\Rightarrow\; \chi \equiv \eta_0-\eta = \int_0^r \frac{\d r'}{\sqrt{1-kr'}}.\label{eq:comoving distance}
\end{equation}
Here, $\chi$ denotes the \textit{comoving distance}. 

Solving the integral in Eq.(\ref{eq:comoving distance}) and isolating $r$, we find three solutions, depending on the curvature 
\begin{align*}
r &= \begin{cases}
\displaystyle \;  \frac{\chi\sin \Big[\sqrt{|\Omega_{k 0}}| H_0 \chi /c\Big]}{\sqrt{|\Omega_{k 0}}| H_0 \chi /c} & \Omega_{k 0} < 0,\\[2mm]
\displaystyle \;\;\chi & \Omega_{k 0} =0, \\[2mm]
\displaystyle \; \frac{\chi\sinh \Big[\sqrt{|\Omega_{k 0}}| H_0 \chi /c\Big] }{\sqrt{|\Omega_{k 0}}| H_0 \chi /c} & \Omega_{k 0} > 0.
\end{cases}
\end{align*}\\


If an object has physical size $D$ and subtends an angle $\theta$ on the sky, the angular diameter distance is defined as $d_A = D/\theta$. Reading off the line element, Eq.(\ref{eq:FLRW in polar coords}), a displacement in the $\theta$-direction corresponds to a proper distance $dD = ar\,d\theta$, so
\begin{align*}
      d_A = D/\theta = ar.
\end{align*}

On the other hand, from the intrinsic luminosity of a source and its observed flux, the luminosity distance $d_L$ can be defined through $F = L/4\pi d_L^2$, which simply reads 
\begin{equation*}
      d_L = a/r = d_A/a^2.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Some cosmological events}

Finally, for our analysis of the simulated data, it is convenient to identify key cosmological events to serve as heuristic markers for our interpretation of the results. We will look at three relevant events at the time scale of the evolution of the background:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=12mm]
      \item matter-radiation equality at $\displaystyle x^{MR}_\text{eq} = \ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{R0}}{\Omega_{M0}}\bigg)\;$;\\
      \item the onset of cosmic acceleration (when $\ddot{a}=0$) at $$\hspace{-8mm} x_\text{acc} = \frac{1}{3}\ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{M0}}{2\Omega_{\Lambda 0}}\bigg)\;;$$
      \item and the matter-dark energy equality at $$ \hspace{-8mm}x^{\Lambda M}_\text{eq} = \frac{1}{3}\ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{M0}}{\Omega_{\Lambda 0}}\bigg).$$
\end{enumerate}
For the derivations of the three $x$'s, see Appendix \ref{appendix:cosmological events}.

\subsection{Implementation details}
\subsubsection{Fiducial cosmological parameters}
Our model Universe will assume $\Lambda$CDM fiducial cosmology and be initialised with the best-fit cosmological parameters from the Planck 2018 data \citep{planck2020}. We will thus take the dimensionless Hubble parameter $h=0.67$, the current day temperature of the CMB radiation $T_\text{CMB0} = 2.7255\text{ K}$, and the effective number of massless neutrino species $N_\text{eff}=3.046$.

For the current day values of the density parameters, we set 
\begin{align*}
      \Omega_{b0} &= 0.05,\\
      \Omega_\text{CDM0} &= 0.267,\\
      \Omega_{K0} &= 0,\\
      \Omega_{\gamma0} &=  \frac{\pi^2}{15} \frac{(k_bT_{\rm CMB 0})^4}{\hbar^3 c^5} \frac{8\pi G} {3H_0^2} \simeq 5.509\times 10^{-5},\\
      \Omega_{\nu 0} &= \frac{7}{8}\left(\frac{4}{11}\right)^{4/3}\hspace{-3mm}N_{\rm eff}\Omega_{\gamma 0} \simeq 3.811 \times 10^{-5},\\[1mm]
      \Omega_{\Lambda0}&= 1-\sum_{i\neq \Lambda} \Omega_{i0} \simeq 0.683.
\end{align*}
Here, the dark energy density parameter is defined such that $\sum_i \Omega_{i0} =1$, to ensure a spatially flat Universe. 

\subsubsection{Numerical integration scheme}
\label{subsub:numerical int scheme}

To evaluate integral quantities such as Eqs.(\ref{eq:t(x)}) and (\ref{eq:eta(x)}), we recast them as ordinary differential equations and integrate numerically using the fourth-order Runge-Kutta solver provided by the GSL library \citep{gsl} and spline the result using the cubic spline algorithm available in GSL.

The cosmic time ODE is
\begin{align*}
      \dd{t}{x} = \frac{1}{H(x)},
\end{align*}
which is to be integrated up from $x_{i}=-\infty$ to some final $x_f$. However, in practice, we must choose a very early time to integrate from. In our case, we solve in the interval $x_i = -20$ up to $x_f=5$. The initial condition for the ODE is determined deep inside the radiation dominated era, when $H(x)\sim H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}e^{-2x}$, such that
\begin{align*}
      \dd{t}{x} = \frac{e^{2x}}{H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}}\;\Rightarrow\;  t(x_i) = \int_{-\infty}^{x_i}  \frac{e^{2x}\d x}{H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}} = \frac{1}{2H(x_i)}.
\end{align*}

Similarly, we solve the ODE for the conformal time 
\begin{align*}
      \dd{\eta}{x} = \frac{c}{\mathcal{H}(x)}
\end{align*}
in the same interval, with the initial condition $\eta(x_i)=c/\mathcal{H}(x_i)$, due to the radiation dominated conformal Hubble function $\mathcal{H}(x) \sim H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}e^{-x}$.

\subsubsection{MCMC parameter fitting}
\label{mile1:imp:MCMC}
Using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), we will test our simulated Universe, based on fiducial cosmological parameters, against observations of type Ia supernova (SNIa) luminosity distances \citep{Betoule_2014}. The procedure searches for the triplet of ``best-fit'' parameters in the $(h,\Omega_{M0}, \Omega_{K0})$ parameter space that minimises

\begin{equation*}
      \chi^2(h,\Omega_{M0}, \Omega_{K0}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{[d_L(z_i,h,\Omega_{M0},\Omega_{K0}) - d_L^{\rm obs}(z_i)]^2 }{\sigma_i^2}.
\end{equation*}
Here, $d_L$ are the predicted luminosity distances from our model at the observed redshifts $z_i$, while $d_L^\text{obs}$ are the observed distances, together with their associated errors.

To perform the actual MCMC parameter fitting procedure, we employ the code written by Hans A. Winther \citep{winther_supernova}.

Displayed in Figure \ref{fig:density parameters} are the density parameters $\Omega_R$, $\Omega_M$, and $\Omega_\Lambda$ as functions of $x$, representing the contributions from relativistic components, non-relativistic matter, and the cosmological constant. The figure highlights three distinct periods in the history of our model Universe:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=12mm, rightmargin=12mm]

This timeline is explained by the fact that $\rho_R\propto a^{-4}$, $\rho_M\propto a^{-3}$, while $\rho_\Lambda =\text{const}$, such that the density of the relativistic components decays faster than non-relativistic matter, which in turn decays faster than the density of dark energy.

The transition between cosmological eras leaves clear signatures in the conformal Hubble function and its derivatives, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:conformal Hubble}. Equations (\ref{eq:dHpdx}) and (\ref{eq:ddHpddx}) predict specific values in each regime: $\mathcal{H}'/\mathcal{H} = -1$ and $\mathcal{H}''/\mathcal{H}=1$ in radiation domination, $\mathcal{H}'/\mathcal{H} = -1/2$ and $\mathcal{H}''/\mathcal{H}=1/4$ in matter domination, and both ratios approaching unity in a $\Lambda$-dominated universe. These predicted features are apparent in the bottom panel of Figure \ref{fig:conformal Hubble}.

The top panel also exhibits the cosmic acceleration after $x_\text{acc}$. Since $\mathcal{H}\equiv aH=\dot{a}$, the conformal Hubble function describes the rate of change of the scale factor with respect to cosmic time. We observe that the slope of $\mathcal{H}$ changes from negative to positive, i.e. $\mathcal{H}'/\mathcal{H}$ becomes positive, at the minimum situated at $x=x_{acc}$. A positive slope means that 
\begin{align*}
      \mathcal{H}' = \dd{\dot{a}}{x} = \dd{t}{x} \ddot{a} >0.
\end{align*}
Since $t(x)$ is manifestly a strictly increasing function, $\d t/\d x$ is strictly positive at all times, such that the sign of $\mathcal{H}'$ depends on the sign of $\ddot{a}$. Therefore, we see that $\ddot{a}>0$ for $x>x_\text{acc}$, so we indeed have late-stage cosmic acceleration.

The MCMC parameter fitting to SNIa luminosity distance data (see Sect.\ref{mile1:imp:MCMC}) yielded the best-fit triplet of parameters
\begin{align*}
      h &= 0.701711,\\
      \Omega_{M0} &= 0.255027,\\
      \Omega_{K0} &= 0.0789514,
\end{align*}
with an associated $\chi^2=29.2799$. The observational data contains $N=31$ data, so we have $\chi^2/N\lesssim 1$, indicating a good fit.

Figure \ref{fig:scatter M Lambda} shows the $(\Omega_{M0}, \Omega_{\Lambda0})$ parameter space sampled during the MCMC procedure, highlighting the regions within $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ of the best-fit parameters (minimum $\chi^2$). The fiducial Planck values lie within the $1\sigma$ contour, though slightly offset from the best-fit point.

The marginalized posterior distributions for $\Omega_{\Lambda0}$ and the Hubble parameter $h$ are presented in Figure \ref{fig:posteriors OmegaL0 and h}, with vertical dotted lines indicating the fiducial Planck values. While the fiducial $\Omega_{\Lambda0}$ falls comfortably within the posterior and close to its best-fit value, the same is not true for $h$. The fiducial value of $h$ lies well outside the $1\sigma$ region of its posterior distribution, with a relative difference of
$$\bigg|\frac{h_\text{best-fit}-h_\text{fiducial}}{h_\text{best-fit}}\bigg|\simeq 4.5\%.$$

This discrepancy is further illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:luminosity distances}, which compares the luminosity distance-redshift relation predicted by our best-fit model to that of the fiducial Planck cosmology, alongside the observed SNIa data. The best-fit parameters clearly provide a superior match to the supernova measurements across the full redshift range, reinforcing that while the Planck cosmology remains our adopted baseline for subsequent calculations, it is less consistent with these late-universe distance indicators.

Together, these results highlight the well-known \textit{Hubble tension}: the persistent disagreement between the value of $H_0$ inferred from early-universe observations (such as the CMB) and that derived from late-universe distance measurements (e.g., supernovae or galaxy surveys). Despite this tension, we will adopt the fiducial Planck $\Lambda$CDM cosmology for the remainder of this work, while noting its inconsistency with the SNIa luminosity distances used in our MCMC analysis.




\section{Recombination history}

Having established the background cosmology, we now turn to the recombination history of the Universe. While the background evolution governs the large-scale expansion, it is the microphysics of the photon-baryon plasma that determines when and how the CMB photons we observe today were released. Three epochs are of particular importance: \textit{recombination}, when free electrons and protons combine to form neutral hydrogen at $z \sim 1100$; \textit{decoupling}, when the photon mean free path exceeds the Hubble horizon and the CMB photons stream freely for the first time; and \textit{reionisation}, when the first generation of stars re-ionises the intergalactic medium at $z \sim 8$. Together, these epochs shape the optical depth of the Universe and leave distinct imprints on the CMB power spectrum. In this section we compute the free electron fraction $X_e$, the optical depth $\tau$, and the visibility function $\tilde{g}$, which together encode the full scattering history of CMB photons. The theory and methodology follow \citet{winther_lectures, winther_milestone2}.

\subsection{Theory}
At early times, the Universe was so hot that it was fully ionised, with photons and baryons tightly coupled through Thomson scattering in a photon-baryon plasma. As the Universe expanded and cooled, free electrons and protons combined to form neutral hydrogen in a process known as \textit{recombination}:
$$e^- + p^+ \rightleftharpoons H + \gamma.$$
Before recombination, the plasma was opaque to photons, which scattered repeatedly off free electrons and could not travel freely. After recombination, the photon mean free path grew to exceed the Hubble horizon, and the photons decoupled from the baryons, i.e. the Universe became transparent. The photons released at this moment of decoupling form the CMB we observe today, making the recombination history central to understanding the origin and properties of the CMB.

The opacity of a medium to radiation is quantified by the \textit{optical depth} $\tau$. A beam of light with initial intensity $I_0$ traversing a medium of optical depth $\tau$ emerges with intensity $I = I_0 e^{-\tau}$, so that $\tau \ll 1$ corresponds to a transparent medium and $\tau \gg 1$ to an opaque one. In cosmology, the dominant source of opacity is Thomson scattering of photons off free electrons, giving
\begin{equation*}
    \tau(\eta) = \int_\eta^{\eta_0} n_e \sigma_T a \, \mathrm{d}\eta',
\end{equation*}
where $n_e$ is the free electron number density, $\sigma_T$ is the Thomson cross-section\footnote{The Thomson scattering cross-section is given by $\displaystyle\sigma_T = \frac{8\pi}{3}\frac{\alpha^2\hbar^2}{m_e^2c^2}$.}, and $a$ is the scale factor. The epoch of last scattering, from which the CMB photons we observe today last scattered, corresponds to $\tau \approx 1$.

In order to calculate the optical depth, we must therefore solve for the electron number density $n_e$, or in practice its proxy, the free electron fraction $X_e \equiv n_e/n_H$, where $n_H$ corresponds to the number density of protons (hydrogen nuclei).

\subsubsection{Electron fraction and number density}
\label{theory:Xe and ne}
In the early Universe, when ionising radiation is still efficient and the plasma remains in thermal equilibrium, the free electron fraction can be determined from the \textit{Saha equation}. As a first approximation, we neglect helium and assume all baryons are protons ($Y_p = 0$), so that the proton number density is
\begin{align*}
      n_H \approx n_b = \frac{\rho_b}{m_H} = \frac{3H_0^2\Omega_{b0}}{8\pi G m_H a^3}.
\end{align*}
Under this assumption, the Saha equation takes the form
\begin{equation}
      \frac{X_e^2}{1-X_e} = \frac{1}{n_b} \left(\frac{m_e k_B T_b}{2\pi \hbar^2}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\epsilon_0/k_BT_b},\label{eq:saha only hydrogen}
\end{equation}
where $m_e$ is the electron mass, $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant, $\epsilon_0 = 13.6\;\mathrm{eV}$ is the ionisation energy of hydrogen, $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, and $T_b$ is the baryon temperature. In principle, $T_b$ and the photon temperature $T_\gamma$ should be evolved separately, but since Compton scattering keeps the two tightly coupled until well after recombination, it is an excellent approximation to set $T_b \simeq T_\gamma = T_\mathrm{CMB,0}/a$ \citep[see appendix]{winther_milestone2}. 

Including helium ($Y_p \neq 0$) complicates the picture, as helium can be both singly and doubly ionised, contributing additional free electrons. We define the helium mass fraction as
\begin{equation*}
      Y_p \equiv \frac{m_{\mathrm{He}}n_{\mathrm{He}}}{m_b n_b} = \frac{4n_{\mathrm{He}}}{n_b},
\end{equation*}
where we have approximated the baryon mass as $m_b \approx m_H$, the hydrogen atom mass, and used the fact that each helium nucleus contains four baryons. It follows that the hydrogen number density is
\begin{equation*}
      n_H = (1-Y_p)\,n_b = (1-Y_p)\frac{3H_0^2\Omega_{b0}}{8\pi G m_H a^3}.
\end{equation*}
To account for the three ionisation states of hydrogen and helium, we introduce the ionisation fractions
\begin{equation*}
u \equiv \frac{n_{\mathrm{He}^+}}{n_{\mathrm{He}}}, \qquad
v \equiv \frac{n_{\mathrm{He}^{2+}}}{n_{\mathrm{He}}}, \qquad
w \equiv \frac{n_{\mathrm{H}^+}}{n_{\mathrm{H}}},
\end{equation*}
each of which satisfies its own set of three Saha equations
\begin{align}
      \frac{u}{1-u-v} &= \frac{2}{n_e}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\chi_0/k_BT_b},\label{eq:saha with Helium 1}\\
      \frac{v}{u}     &= \frac{4}{n_e}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\chi_1/k_BT_b},\label{eq:saha with Helium 2}\\
      \frac{w}{1-w}   &= \frac{1}{n_e}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\epsilon_0/k_BT_b}.\label{eq:saha with Helium 3}
\end{align}
Here $\chi_0 = 24.587\;\mathrm{eV}$ is the ionisation energy of neutral helium and $\chi_1 = 4\epsilon_0 = 54.418\;\mathrm{eV}$ is the ionisation energy of $\mathrm{He}^+$. By tallying the free electrons contributed by each ion species, we can define the quantity
\begin{equation}
      f_e \equiv \frac{n_e}{n_b} = \left(2v + u\right)\frac{Y_p}{4} + w\,(1-Y_p),\label{eq:f_e}
\end{equation}
from which the free electron fraction follows as $X_e = f_e/(1-Y_p)$. The factor of two in $2v$ reflects the fact that each doubly ionised helium nucleus contributes two free electrons.\\

The Saha approximation is valid as long as the plasma remains in thermal equilibrium, which holds well for $X_e \gtrsim 0.99$. As recombination proceeds and the Universe departs from equilibrium, the Saha equation overestimates the recombination rate and a non-equilibrium treatment is required. The \textit{Peebles equation} \citep{peebles} provides such a treatment, tracking the non-equilibrium evolution of $X_e$ through the ODE
\begin{equation*}
      \frac{\mathrm{d}X_e}{\mathrm{d}x} = \frac{C_r(T_b)}{H} \left[\beta(T_b)(1-X_e) - n_H \alpha^{(2)}(T_b)X_e^2\right]. \label{eq:Peebles}
\end{equation*}
The two terms in brackets represent the competing processes of photoionisation (first term), which increases $X_e$, and recombination (second term), which decreases it. The prefactor $C_r$ accounts for the fact that recombinations directly to the ground state produce an ionising photon that immediately re-ionises a nearby hydrogen atom, making such recombinations ineffective. Only recombinations to excited states, followed by either the two-photon $2s\rightarrow 1s$ decay or escape of a Lyman-$\alpha$ photon, result in a net increase of neutral hydrogen. The various quantities appearing in the Peebles equation are
\begin{align*}
      C_r(T_b) &= \frac{\Lambda_{2s\rightarrow1s} + \Lambda_{\alpha}}{\Lambda_{2s\rightarrow1s} + \Lambda_{\alpha} + \beta^{(2)}(T_b)},\\
      \Lambda_{2s\rightarrow1s} &= 8.227 \;\mathrm{s}^{-1},\\
      \Lambda_{\alpha} &= \frac{H}{(8\pi)^2 n_{1s}}\left(\frac{3\epsilon_0}{\hbar c}\right)^3,\\
      n_{1s} &= (1-X_e)n_H,\\
      \beta^{(2)}(T_b) &= \beta(T_b)\, e^{3\epsilon_0/4k_BT_b},\\
      \beta(T_b) &= \alpha^{(2)}(T_b) \left(\frac{m_ek_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\epsilon_0/k_BT_b},\\
      \alpha^{(2)}(T_b) &= \frac{8\sigma_T c}{\sqrt{3\pi}}\sqrt{\frac{\epsilon_0}{k_BT_b}}\,\phi_2(T_b),\\
      \phi_2(T_b) &= 0.448\ln\!\left(\epsilon_0/k_BT_b\right).
\end{align*}
Here $\Lambda_{2s\rightarrow1s}$ is the decay rate of the $2s$ state via two-photon emission, $\Lambda_\alpha$ is the Lyman-$\alpha$ escape rate, $\alpha^{(2)}$ is the recombination coefficient to excited states ($n\geq 2$), $\beta$ is the corresponding photoionisation rate, and $\beta^{(2)}$ is the photoionisation rate from the $2s$ state. 




\subsubsection{Reionisation}

Around a redshift of $z \sim 10$, the first stars and galaxies form and begin emitting large amounts of high-energy ultraviolet radiation. This radiation is energetic enough to ionise the surrounding neutral hydrogen and helium, driving the free electron fraction $X_e$ back up from its post-recombination value of $X_e \sim 10^{-4}$ toward values close to unity. This process is known as \textit{reionisation}. Our primary interest is its effect on CMB photons propagating from the surface of last scattering to us today. For this purpose a simple phenomenological model suffices. We will thus use the same simplified model used in CAMB \citet{Lewis:1999bs}, adding two tanh-shaped transitions to the Peebles solution
\begin{align}
      X_e = X_e^{\mathrm{Peebles}} &+ \frac{1+f_{\mathrm{He}}}{2}\left(1+\tanh\frac{y_{\mathrm{reion}}-y}{\Delta y_{\mathrm{reion}}}\right) \nonumber\\
                                    &+\quad \frac{f_{\mathrm{He}}}{2}\left(1 + \tanh \frac{z_{\mathrm{Hereion}}-z}{\Delta z_{\mathrm{Hereion}}}\right).\label{eq:reionisation}
\end{align}
The first term accounts for the reionisation of hydrogen and the second for the reionisation of helium. Each transition is centred on its respective reionisation redshift and smoothed over a width $\Delta z$. Here $y \equiv (1+z)^{3/2} = e^{-3x/2}$ is a rescaled redshift variable, $f_{\mathrm{He}} \equiv Y_p / [4(1-Y_p)]$ is the helium-to-hydrogen number ratio, $y_{\mathrm{reion}} = (1+z_{\mathrm{reion}})^{3/2}$, and $\Delta y_{\mathrm{reion}} = \tfrac{3}{2}\sqrt{1+z_{\mathrm{reion}}}\,\Delta z_{\mathrm{reion}}$ is the corresponding width in $y$-space. From the assumed Planck cosmology, we have $z_\text{reion}=8$, $\Delta z_\text{reion}=0.5$, $z_\text{Hereion}=3.5$, and $\Delta z_\text{Hereion}=0.5$. 

\subsubsection{Visibility function}
After we have solved for the optical depth $\tau$, we will also compute the \textit{visibility function} $\tilde{g}$ given by 
\begin{align}
      \tilde{g}(x) = -\tau'(x)e^{-\tau(x)}.\label{eq:visibility function}
\end{align}
It describes the probability density for a given photon to last time having scattered at time $x$, and has the property $\int_{-\infty}^0 \tilde{g}(x)\d x = 1$. The visibility function peaks when the photons decoupled from the baryons, i.e. at the transition between the opaque and transparent epochs (taken to be when $\tau = 1$). 

\subsubsection{Sound horizon}
The final quantity of interest is the \textit{sound horizon} at decoupling which is the total comoving distance a sound wave in the photon-baryon plasma of the early Universe could travel from the Big Bang until photons decouple. This scale length is the maximum distance between regions that could have had causal contact and will thus leave a distinct imprint on both the CMB power spectrum and the large-scale distribution of matter in the Universe. The sound speed of the tightly coupled photon-baryon plasma is somewhat lower than the relativistic sound speed of a pure photon gas ($c/\sqrt{3}$), and is given by
\begin{equation*}
    c_s = \frac{c\sqrt{R}}{\sqrt{3(1+R)}}, \qquad R \equiv \frac{4\Omega_{\gamma 0}}{3\Omega_{b0}a}.
\end{equation*}
The sound horizon is then
\begin{equation}
    s(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} \frac{c_s}{\mathcal{H}} \, \mathrm{d}x',\quad\Rightarrow\quad \dd{s}{x} = \frac{c_s}{\mathcal{H}(x)}, \label{eq:sound horizon}
\end{equation}
and its value at decoupling $s(x_\text{dec})$ sets the physical scale of the acoustic peaks in the CMB.

\subsection{Implementation details}
\subsubsection{Solving for the free electron fraction}
As outlined in Section~\ref{theory:Xe and ne}, the evolution of the free electron fraction $X_e$ is divided into three distinct epochs, each requiring a different treatment; the equilibrium Saha regime at early times, the out-of-equilibrium Peebles regime during and after recombination, and the reionisation epoch at late times. The calculation of $X_e$ is therefore done in three phases:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=12mm, rightmargin=12mm]
      \item first we calculate $X_e$ in Saha regime, using either Eq.(\ref{eq:saha only hydrogen}) or Eqs.(\ref{eq:saha with Helium 1}) to (\ref{eq:saha with Helium 3}) depending on if we include helium or not, over the whole time interval, spanning from $x_\text{start}=\ln(10^{-8})$ up to $x_\text{end} = 0$;\\
      \item then we search through the Saha solution and identify when the approximation breaks down (i.e. when $X_e<0.99$) and start solving the Peebles ODE, Eq.(\ref{eq:Peebles}), from this point onwards, replacing the values from the Saha solution along the way;\\
      \item lastly, we the reionisation contribution at late times to the Peebles solution, as described by Eq.(\ref{eq:reionisation}), and spline the result at the end.
\end{enumerate}

The hydrogen-only Saha equation, Eq.~(\ref{eq:saha only hydrogen}), reduces to a simple quadratic in $X_e$, which can be solved analytically. Retaining the physically meaningful positive root, we obtain
\begin{equation*}
      X_e = \frac{-A_s + A_s\sqrt{1 + 4/A_s}}{2},
\end{equation*}
where $A_s(T_b)$ is just the RHS of Eq.(\ref{eq:saha only hydrogen}). Going far back in time, the baryon temperature will get extremely large such that $A_s\sim T_b^{3/2}$ blows up and may cause overflows. To get around this problem we Taylor expand the square root term\footnote{$\sqrt{1+x} \simeq 1 + {x}/{2}$ when $|x|\ll 1$.} and find that $X_e =1$ in this limit. This is an excellent approximation as long as $A_s \gg 4$. In our numerical implementation, the approximate solution is imposed when $A_s > 400$.\\

If however we include helium in our Universe ($Y_p\neq 0$), we must instead solve the three Saha equations, Eqs.(\ref{eq:saha with Helium 1}) to (\ref{eq:saha with Helium 3}). Defining the quantities
\begin{align*}
      r_0\equiv& \frac{2}{n_b}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\chi_0/k_BT_b},\\
      r_1\equiv& \frac{4}{n_b}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\chi_1/k_BT_b},\\
      r_2\equiv& \frac{1}{n_b}\left(\frac{m_e k_BT_b}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\epsilon_0/k_BT_b},
\end{align*}
we can solve for the ion matter fractions $u,\; v$, and $w$ as given by
\begin{align*}
      u &= \frac{f_er_0}{f_e^2 + f_er_0 + r_0r_1},\\
      v &= \frac{r_0r_1}{f_e^2 + f_er_0 + r_0r_1},\\
      w &= \frac{r_2}{f_e + r_2}.
\end{align*}
Numerically, the system is solved iteratively at each time step. Beginning with an initial guess of $f_e = 1$, we solve for the ionisation fractions $u$, $v$, and $w$ from the three Saha equations, then update $f_e$ using Eq.~(\ref{eq:f_e}). This cycle is repeated until convergence, defined by $|f_{e,\mathrm{new}} - f_{e,\mathrm{old}}| < 10^{-10}$, which is typically achieved within a handful of iterations. The free electron fraction then follows as $X_e = f_e / (1 - Y_p)$.

Going into the Peebles regime, we set the initial condition of Eq.(\ref{eq:Peebles}) to be the value of $X_e$ from the last valid Saha solution to ensure continuity in the full solution. The only thing worth noting about the numerical implementation is that $\beta^{(2)}$ is set to zero when $\epsilon_0/k_BT_b \gg 1$ to avoid numerical overflow from the exponential factor. Specifically, we set $\beta^{(2)}=0$ when $\epsilon_0/k_BT_b>200$.

Finally, introducing the reionisation contributions from hydrogen and helium reionisation is as simple as adding the extra tanh-terms in Eq.(\ref{eq:reionisation}). After that is done, we spline the resulting function $X_e$ and $n_e$ in log-space using cubic spline, since their values span several orders of magnitude within short time frames.


\subsubsection{Calculating the optical depth and visibility function}
Once we have solved for the free electron fraction, and have thus also by proxy calculated the free electron number density $n_e$, we calculate the optical depth from its differential form 
\begin{align*}
      \tau' = \dd{\tau}{x} = -\frac{c\sigma_Tn_e(x)}{H(x)},
\end{align*}
imposing the intial condition $\tau(0)=0$. This is achieved by solving the ODE backwards in time from $x_\text{end}=0$ to $x_\text{start}$. We spline the result and its derivative, and calculate its second derivative by differentiating the $\tau'$ using the method built into our spline routine \citep{winther_spline}. 

The visibility function is then calculated using Eq.(\ref{eq:visibility function}). Splining $\tilde{g}$, we then its first two derivatives, $\tilde{g}'$ and $\tilde{g}''$, employing the same differentiating scheme for cubic splines.

\subsubsection{Calculating the sound horizon at decoupling}
To get the sound horizon as a function of $x$, we solve the ODE stated in Eq.(\ref{eq:sound horizon}) using the usual method. The initial condition is found deep in the radiation dominated era of the Universe, when $\mathcal{H}(x)\sim H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}e^{-x}$ and $R\to \infty$. The sound speed of the photon-baryon plasma is slowly varying, and approximately constant, since 
\begin{equation*}
      \lim_{x\to-\infty} c_s(x) = \frac{c}{\sqrt{3}}\lim_{R\to\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+1/R}} = \frac{c}{\sqrt{3}}.
\end{equation*}
We then get the initial condition
\begin{equation*}
      s(x_{start}) = \frac{1}{H_0\sqrt{\Omega_{R0}}}\int_{-\infty}^{x_\text{start}}\hspace{-3mm}c_s(x)\;e^x\d x = \frac{c_s(x_\text{start})}{\mathcal{H}(x_\text{start})}.
\end{equation*}

After splining the result, we plug in the value of $x$ at the time of decoupling, defined as when $\tau(x_\text{dec}) = 1$ or $\tilde{g}$ is maximal, to get the sound horizon at decoupling $r_s\equiv s(x_\text{dec})$.

\subsubsection{Sound horizon at decoupling}

Evaluating the sound horizon at decoupling $r_s$ for the fiducial model, i.e. at $x_\mathrm{dec} = -6.9728$ (corresponding to redshift $z_\mathrm{dec} = 1066.23$), we find
\begin{equation*}
    r_s \equiv s(x_\mathrm{dec}) \simeq 146.501\;\mathrm{Mpc}.
\end{equation*}
This value is in good agreement with the standard result from \textit{Planck} \citep{planck2020}, and sets a fundamental physical scale in the Universe. At decoupling there is no longer any radiative pressure to counteract the gravitational collapse of the baryons, so the acoustic oscillations in the photon-baryon plasma are frozen in place, and the sound horizon marks the maximum distance over which these oscillations could have coherently developed. This scale is directly imprinted as the characteristic angular separation of the acoustic peaks in the CMB power spectrum, and as the baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) scale in the matter power spectrum.

\section{Derivatives of the conformal Hubble function}
\label{appendix:derivatives of Hp}

The Hubble function is given by
\begin{equation*}
H = H_0 \sqrt{(\Omega_{b0}+\Omega_{\rm CDM 0})a^{-3} + (\Omega_{\gamma 0} + \Omega_{\nu 0}) a^{-4} + \Omega_{k 0} a^{-2} + \Omega_{\Lambda 0}},
\end{equation*}
where we can define $\Omega_{M0}\equiv \Omega_{b0}+\Omega_{\rm CDM 0}$ and $\Omega_{R0}\equiv \Omega_{\gamma 0} + \Omega_{\nu 0}$. The first derivative of the conformal Hubble function $\mathcal{H}\equiv aH(a)$ with respect to $x\equiv\ln a$ is
\begin{align*}
      \dd{\mathcal{H}}{x} &= \dd{a}{x}H + a \dd{H}{x} = \mathcal{H} + a^2 \dd{H}{a}.
\end{align*}
Let us compute the derivative of $H(a)$:
\begin{align*}
      \dd{H}{a} = -\frac{H_0^2}{2H}\Big(3\Omega_{M0}a^{-4} + 4\Omega_{R 0} a^{-5} + 2\Omega_{k 0} a^{-3}\Big).
\end{align*}
This gives 
\begin{align*}
      \dd{\mathcal{H}}{x} &= \mathcal{H} - a^2 \frac{H_0^2}{2H}\Big(3\Omega_{M0}a^{-4} + 4\Omega_{R 0} a^{-5} + 2\Omega_{k 0} a^{-3} \Big)\\
      &= \mathcal{H}\bigg[1- \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{M} -2\Omega_{R} - \Omega_K\bigg].
\end{align*}
Here the $\Omega_i$'s refer to the density parameters as functions of scale factor, i.e. $\Omega_i(a)$, which should be evaluated at $a=e^x.$\\

The second derivative is a bit more cumbersome to calculate; from the product rule it follows that 
\begin{align*}
      \dd{^2\mathcal{H}}{x^2} =& \;\mathcal{H}\bigg[1- \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{m} -2\Omega_{r} - \Omega_K\bigg]^2\\
      &+ \mathcal{H}\dd{}{x}\bigg[1- \frac{3}{2}\Omega_{m} -2\Omega_{r} - \Omega_K\bigg].
\end{align*}
Let $B$ be the expression inside the big bracket. We thus need $\d B/\d x$, which is most easily calculated by factoring out $H_0^2/H(a)^2$ from the last three terms of $B$;
\begin{align*}
      \dd{B}{x} =& -\frac{a}{2}\dd{}{a}\Bigg(\frac{H_0^2}{H^2}\Bigg)\Big(3\Omega_{M0}a^{-3} + 4\Omega_{R0}a^{-4} + 2\Omega_{K0}a^{-2} \Big)\\
      &-\frac{a}{2}\frac{H_0^2}{H^2}\dd{}{a}\Big(3\Omega_{M0}a^{-3} + 4\Omega_{R0}a^{-4} + 2\Omega_{K0}a^{-2} \Big)\\[3mm]
      =& -\frac{1}{2}\Big(3\Omega_M +4\Omega_R +2\Omega_K\Big)^2 + \frac{1}{2}\Big(9\Omega_M + 16 \Omega_R + 4 \Omega_K\Big).
\end{align*}
From this we end up with second derivative
\begin{align*}
      \dd{^2\mathcal{H}}{x^2} =&\; \mathcal{H}\Bigg\{\bigg[1-\frac{3}{2}\Omega_M -2\Omega_R  -\Omega_K\bigg]^2\\
      & -\frac{1}{2}\Big(3\Omega_M + 4\Omega_R + 2\Omega_K \Big)^2+\frac{1}{2}\Big(9\Omega_M +16\Omega_R +4\Omega_K\Big)\Bigg\} \;\Bigg|_{a = e^x}.
\end{align*}


\section{Logarithmic scale factor at different cosmological events}
\label{appendix:cosmological events}

Matter-radiation equality happens when $\Omega_M(a^{MR}_\text{eq})=\Omega_R(a^{MR}_\text{eq})$. Using the expressions given in Eq.(\ref{eq:density parameter evolutions}), we find 
\begin{equation*}
      \frac{\Omega_{M0}}{(a^{MR}_\text{eq})^3} = \frac{\Omega_{R0}}{(a^{MR}_\text{eq})^4}\; \Rightarrow\; x^{MR}_\text{eq}\equiv \ln a^{MR}_\text{eq} = \ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{R0}}{\Omega_{M0}}\bigg)\;.
\end{equation*}
In a similar way, we derive that the matter-dark energy equality occurs for
\begin{align*}
      \frac{\Omega_{M0}}{(a^{\Lambda M}_\text{eq})^3} = \Omega_{\Lambda0}\; \Rightarrow\; x^{\Lambda M}_\text{eq}\equiv \ln a^{\Lambda M}_\text{eq} = \frac{1}{3}\ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{M0}}{\Omega_{\Lambda0}}\bigg)\;.
\end{align*}

The onset of cosmic acceleration is defined to be when $\ddot{a}=0$. From the second Friedmann equation, Eq.(\ref{eq:Friedmann 2nd}), we get that this occurs whenever
\begin{align*}
      \sum_i \rho_i(a_\text{acc})(1+3w_i) = 0.
\end{align*}
The relevant equations of state are $w_M = 0$, $w_R = 1/3$ and $w_\Lambda = -1$. We neglect the curvature parameter when working with fiducial cosmological parameter, and thus the condition for cosmic acceleration can be recast into 
\begin{align*}
      \Omega_M + 2\Omega_R -2\Omega_\Lambda =0\\
      \Rightarrow\quad \frac{\Omega_{M0}}{(a_\text{acc})^3} + \frac{2\Omega_{R0}}{(a_\text{acc})^4}-2\Omega_{\Lambda0} = 0.
\end{align*}
At late times, the radiation density parameter can be neglected, so we end up with
\begin{align*}
      x_\text{acc} \equiv \ln a_\text{acc} = \frac{1}{3}\ln\bigg(\frac{\Omega_{M0}}{2\Omega_{\Lambda 0}}\bigg)\;.
\end{align*}
